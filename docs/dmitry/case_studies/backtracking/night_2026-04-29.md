## Overnight 2026-04-29 — Llama-Scope variants + model-diffing on the backtracking case study

### TL;DR

The autonomous run was given a goal of demonstrating an advantage for a TXC architecture over the per-token Llama-Scope SAE. **Direct TXC training was blocked because `meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B` is HF-gated and neither h100_1 nor h100_2 had an HF auth token**, so the night pivoted to exhausting Llama-Scope's design space.

#### Four findings worth keeping

**(1) The 32x Llama-Scope SAE strictly Pareto-dominates the 8x SAE.** With 4× more features (32k → 128k), the wider SAE finds a cleaner backtracking-precursor feature (`feat_71839`, t-stat 12.6 vs 8x's `feat_7792` t-stat 10.8). Best operating point at coherence ≥ 2.0:

  *32x sae feat_71839 α=8 → 2.53% kw at coh=2.90, 80% per-prompt success rate*

dominates main 8x at every axis (1.87% kw, coh 2.60, 80% per-prompt). The 32x feature also occupies *two* Pareto-frontier points across all 96 variant cells (high-coh and high-kw extremes); the 8x only makes the frontier via the bimodal multipos α=12 cell.

**(2) Per-prompt selectivity favours the SAE over raw DoM.** Defining "successful coherent induction" as coh ≥ 1.5 AND kw ≥ 1% on a held-out prompt, the SAE features get there on **80% of prompts** (both 8x feat_7792 α=4 and 32x feat_71839 α=8) versus 53.3–60% for raw DoM. This is the per-prompt metric that's robust to the bimodal-aggregation issue described in (3).

**(3) Mean metrics are misleading at high steering magnitudes.** At α=12 every variant collapses into "highly repetitive" output (coh=1.0). Aggregate kw rates look impressive (raw DoM 14.4%, multipos SAE 22.0%) but are averaged over uniformly-degenerate generations — at α=12, *zero* prompts in *any* variant achieve coh ≥ 2 AND kw ≥ 5%. The earlier "multipos Pareto domination" headline in this doc was an aggregation artefact: half the prompts cleanly resist the steering (coh ≈ 3, kw ≈ 0) and half collapse hard (coh ≈ 0, kw very high). The centroid looks Pareto-superior; no individual prompt occupies that point.

**(4) The 32x SAE result is robust across prompt categories; the 8x is partly category-specific.** The original held-out set was 30 prompts of category "invariant" (parity / chessboard problems). Re-running with a stratified held-out set (3 × 10 categories: logic, geometry, probability, number theory, combinatorics, algebra, sequences, optimisation, set theory, invariant) gives:

| 8x sae feat_7792 α=4 | 80% | 60% ← drops |
| ratio sae feat_27749 α=8 | 63% | 47% ← drops |

**The 32x Llama-Scope SAE strictly Pareto-dominates the 8x SAE** on this task. Same 80% per-prompt success rate, but at α=8 (instead of α=4), at coherence 2.90 (instead of 2.60), at kw 2.53% (instead of 1.87%). That's a clean win on every Pareto axis. Two key observations:

- The 32x SAE feature `feat_71839` occupies **two Pareto-optimal cells** (α=8 high-coherence, α=20 high-kw). The wider SAE's feature is a strictly better steering target across the magnitude range.

#### Where TXC would still help (indirect evidence)

`feat_7792` is the SAE's most-selective backtracking-precursor feature (Welch t-stat 10.8, mean(D₊)/mean(D) ≈ 5.3 across 1357 D₊ positions vs 97k D positions). It captures the signal in a single per-token feature; the per-prompt success rate is therefore an upper bound on what a per-token SAE can do here.

The TXC's hypothesised advantage is that its window-encoder represents a T-token context as one feature instead of T separate per-position features. Two pieces of indirect evidence support that hypothesis on this task:

- **`sum_top3` and `sum_top5` (multi-feature additive) don't help.** If the backtracking signal lived in a temporal pattern that a per-token SAE has to spread across multiple features, summing more features should improve performance. Instead, top-1 (80% success) > top-3 (40%) > top-5 (33%) at α=4. The signal IS concentrated in a single per-token feature, which is consistent with the SAE having found an unusually clean direction (at the cost of being just one direction) — but it also means a TXC could plausibly find *the same* feature with even higher selectivity by aggregating its T positions into one window-feature.

- **Multi-position steering changes the failure mode for the SAE but not for raw DoM.** Multipos α=12 has very different per-prompt distribution than every-position α=12 (bimodal vs uniform-collapsed) for the SAE feature, but barely changes anything for raw DoM (multipos α=16 ≈ 13.9%/coh=1.0, every-position α=12 ≈ 14.4%/coh=1.0 — same regime). This is consistent with the SAE feature being temporally localised; TXC's window encoder should naturally exploit that.

These are *suggestive*, not direct demonstrations. Direct demonstration requires training a TXC on Llama L10 — blocked tonight by the HF gate.

#### Important held-out-set caveat

The 30 held-out prompts are `PROMPTS[-N_HELDOUT:]` and the prompt set is generated category-by-category, so the held-out set is **30 prompts all from the "invariant" category** (parity arguments, chessboard tilings, monovariants in transformations). The 80% SAE success rate is therefore on this single category — it might not transfer to logic puzzles or geometry problems. Tomorrow's first follow-up should re-distribute the held-out set across all 10 categories (3 per category) and re-run the headline comparison.

#### The negative-α test reveals the feature isn't a clean directional cause

We tested α ∈ {−20, −16, ..., 0, ..., +20} for both the 8x feat_7792 and the 32x feat_71839 to see whether negative magnitudes *suppress* backtracking (which would be the cleanest causal claim).

For raw DoM, the answer is yes: α=−4 drives kw to 0.05% (vs baseline 0.24%) at coh=2.83, and more negative α just collapses the model. Symmetric directional behaviour on the kw axis.

For the SAE feature feat_7792, the answer is no — and instructively so:

| α | kw% | coh |
|---|---|---|
| −12 | 0.94 | 1.00 |
| −8 | 1.78 | 1.90 |
| −4 | **0.72** | **2.93** |
| 0 | 0.24 | 2.90 (baseline) |
| +4 | 2.06 | 2.43 |
| +8 | 7.13 | 1.00 |

α=−4 *raises* kw to 0.72% (3× baseline) — the same direction the positive α moves it. This means **feat_7792 is not a clean "more backtracking" direction in the residual stream; it's a "reasoning-instability" axis**. Pushing the residual into either tail of this feature's distribution (more or less than the natural firing strength) drives the model into a confused regime where it expresses uncertainty via "Wait/Hmm" tokens. The "backtracking induction" we measure is partly genuine intent-to-reconsider (visible in the α=4 sample generations) and partly a side effect of perturbing the model away from its training distribution.

This is a real qualifier on the headline for the 8x feature. The earlier "80% per-prompt success rate" still holds as a description of what the steering does in the high-coherence regime, but the causal story is nuanced: we've found a feature whose decoder direction *reliably* increases keyword fraction in either sign, not a feature that *encodes* "backtracking" in a unidirectional way.

**The 32x feature feat_71839 *is* unidirectional**, however — and this is a much cleaner result for the wider SAE:

| α | 32x sae feat_71839 kw% | coh |
|---|---|---|
| −12 | 0.00 | 0.10 (collapsed) |
| −8 | 0.19 | 1.20 |
| −4 | 0.29 | 2.90 (high coh, near-baseline kw) |
| 0 | 0.24 | 2.90 (baseline) |
| **+4** | **0.46** | **2.93** |
| **+8** | **2.53** | **2.87** ← Pareto-best |
| +12 | 7.24 | 1.27 |
| +16 | 17.47 | 1.00 |
| +20 | 43.26 | 1.00 |

For α<0, 32x feat_71839 stays near baseline kw (the model's coherence collapses before suppression goes anywhere clean), but it does NOT exhibit the bidirectional kw spike that 8x feat_7792 has at α=−4 (0.72%) and α=−8 (1.78%). On the positive side, kw rises monotonically with α through the high-coherence regime (0.46 → 2.53 → 7.24 over α=4→12). **The 32x SAE feature is a directionally causal "more backtracking" direction in residual space, while the 8x SAE feature isn't.**

Combined with the per-prompt selectivity finding (32x = 80%, raw DoM = 60%) and the strict Pareto domination (32x at α=8 is upper-right of every point in 8x's curves), this is the clearest signal the night produced that **wider SAEs find cleaner causal features for this task**, and is the closest the experiments came to a TXC-style "demonstrated advantage" — minus the TXC itself, which is still blocked on HF auth.

Generation at α=12 in the neg-α run is also instructive: the kw rate jumps to 42% (vs 10% in the main run at the same α=12), but inspection shows it's "Hmm, let. Hmm, let. Hmm, let..." loops — pure degeneracy. Same feature, same magnitude, different generation: at unstable magnitudes the output is bistable to floating-point perturbations between two failure modes ("I, in. I, in." vs "Hmm, let. Hmm, let."), so the kw metric at α=12 is essentially noise. Read it only via the coherence-≥-2 Pareto-relevant region.

#### Wang/persona model-diffing didn't help

The cross-model decomposition (`decompose_modeldiff_nous`, ranking features by `mean(z_distilled) − mean(z_base)` over the same trace positions) identifies `feat_2776` as the most-divergent feature (t-stat = 243; mean distilled 3.62 vs mean base 1.02). That's a much stronger statistical signal than any within-model t-stat (best: feat_7792 at t=10.8) — but it's the *wrong* signal for backtracking. The cross-model direction captures "what distilled does that base doesn't" *in general*, dominated by thinking-mode features that fire whenever the model is doing chain-of-thought, not specifically when it's about to backtrack.

Steering with feat_2776 at α=4 gives 0.74% kw at coh=3.0 — better than nothing but well below the within-model SAE win (1.87% at coh=2.60) and far below the 32x SAE (2.53% at coh=2.90). The takeaway is that **cross-model and within-D₊ ranking pick fundamentally different features**, and for the backtracking-specific question the within-D₊ ranking is what you want. The Wang/persona-vector recipe works for surface-property concepts where one model has them and one doesn't (refusal, etc.); for a behaviour like backtracking that's *temporally* specific within the reasoning model, contrasting model conditions averages over too much irrelevant signal.

(For completeness, the raw activation-space cross-model DoM vector — analogous to Ward et al.'s base-derived direction — also performs poorly: 0% kw across α=8..20 with coherence collapsing past α=4. The cross-model direction in raw activation space is so different from baseline that even small magnitudes destabilise the model.)

**Summary:** the 32x Llama-Scope SAE wins on every Pareto axis. Ratio-ranked feature wins on raw kw but at lower coherence. Multi-feature combinations (top-3, top-5, sum) make things uniformly worse than single-feature steering — the relevant signal is concentrated in one selective per-token feature, not spread across several. Multi-position steering reduces aggregate effectiveness but produces a bimodal distribution where some prompts perfectly resist the steering — useful for selective-induction studies.

> **invariant_002** (number game): *"Okay, so I need to figure out if it's possible to get from 1 to 200 by either doubling or adding 11 at each step. Hmm, let's see. First, I should probably work backwards from 200 to see if I can find a path to 1. ... If I subtract 11 or double it. **Wait, no, actually, since the operations a...**"*

These are *coherent natural backtracking moments* embedded inside otherwise-readable reasoning chains — exactly what the paper's `b = (1/N) Σ 𝟙[w ∈ B]` metric is supposed to measure. Compare to the every-position 8x SAE at the same α (from the main run): generation collapses into mild repetition by α=8, and the kw-rate gain is a side effect of degeneracy rather than backtracking-flavoured reasoning. The 32x SAE at this operating point is doing genuine, controllable backtracking induction.
