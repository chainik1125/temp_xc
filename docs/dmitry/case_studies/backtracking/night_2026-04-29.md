## Overnight 2026-04-29 — Llama-Scope variants + model-diffing on the backtracking case study

### TL;DR

The autonomous run was given a goal of demonstrating an advantage for a TXC architecture over the per-token Llama-Scope SAE. **Direct TXC training was blocked because `meta-llama/Llama-3.1-8B` is HF-gated and neither h100_1 nor h100_2 had an HF auth token**, so the night pivoted to exhausting Llama-Scope's design space.

#### Two findings worth keeping

**(1) Per-prompt selectivity favours the SAE.** Out of 30 held-out prompts at α=4 (the highest-coherence non-trivial steering point), the **single SAE feature feat_7792 successfully induces coherent backtracking-flavoured reasoning** (coh ≥ 1.5 AND kw ≥ 1%) on **80% of prompts** (24/30). The raw-DoM baseline at the same magnitude does so on 53% (16/30). That's a clean SAE win on a per-prompt metric — not the easily-confounded mean-kw/mean-coherence aggregate — and is the closest we got to a defensible "SAE > DoM" story on this task.

**(2) Mean metrics are misleading at high steering magnitudes.** At α=12 every variant collapses into "highly repetitive" output (coh=1.0). The mean kw rate at that point looks impressive (raw DoM 14.4%, multipos SAE 22.0%) but it's averaged over uniformly degenerate generations: at α=12, *zero* prompts in *any* variant achieve coherent (coh ≥ 2) backtracking with kw ≥ 5%. The "Pareto-superior" multipos finding I reported earlier in this doc is real as an aggregate but stems from a bimodal distribution — about half the prompts cleanly preserve their reasoning (coh ≈ 3, kw ≈ 0) and half collapse into "Hmm, let. Hmm, let." loops (coh ≈ 0, kw very high). The averaged centroid looks like a Pareto-dominant point that no individual prompt actually occupies. Be careful before reading the multipos result as "strict Pareto domination".

| Operating point | mean kw% | mean coh | per-prompt success rate (coh ≥ 1.5, kw ≥ 1%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **32x sae feat_71839 α=8** | **2.5** | **2.90** | **80.0% (24/30)** ← Pareto-best of the night |
| ratio sae feat_27749 α=8 | 2.6 | 2.27 | 63.3% (19/30) |
| **main sae feat_7792 α=4** | 1.9 | 2.60 | 80.0% (24/30) |
| 32x raw_dom α=4 | 1.3 | 3.00 | 60.0% (18/30) |
| ratio raw_dom α=4 | 1.3 | 3.00 | 60.0% |
| main raw_dom α=4 | 1.1 | 3.00 | 53.3% (16/30) |
| topk5 sum_top5 α=8 | _ | _ | 50.0% (15/30) |

**The 32x Llama-Scope SAE strictly Pareto-dominates the 8x SAE** on this task. Same 80% per-prompt success rate, but at α=8 (instead of α=4), at coherence 2.90 (instead of 2.60), at kw 2.53% (instead of 1.87%). That's a clean win on every Pareto axis. The relevant feature is `feat_71839` (t-stat 12.6 vs 8x's `feat_7792` t-stat 10.8). With 4× more features (32k → 128k), the wider SAE finds a better-targeted backtracking direction.

**Ranking by `mean(D₊)/mean(D)` ratio also helps** (`feat_27749` ratio=11.5×, vs the t-stat-best `feat_7792` ratio=5.3×). The ratio-ranked feature steers higher kw (2.63% at coh=2.27) but at lower coherence than the 32x finding. Different trade-off; both clean SAE wins.

#### Cross-variant Pareto frontier

Reducing every (variant, mode, target, magnitude) cell to one (kw, coherence) point and computing the non-dominated set across all 60 cells gives 7 Pareto-optimal points:

| variant | target | mode | α | kw% | coh |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 32x | raw_dom | raw_dom | 4.0 | 1.26 | 3.00 |
| ratio | raw_dom | raw_dom | 4.0 | 1.26 | 3.00 |
| **32x** | **feat_71839** | **sae_additive** | **8** | **2.53** | **2.90** |
| ratio | feat_27749 | sae_additive | 8 | 2.63 | 2.27 |
| multipos | raw_dom | raw_dom | 8 | 6.92 | 1.90 |
| multipos | feat_7792 | sae_additive | 12 | 22.02 | 1.30 |
| **32x** | **feat_71839** | **sae_additive** | **20** | **43.26** | **1.00** |

Two key observations:

- The 32x SAE feature `feat_71839` occupies **two Pareto-optimal cells** (α=8 high-coherence, α=20 high-kw). The 8x SAE's `feat_7792` does not appear on the frontier at any high-coherence point — it only makes it via the bimodal multipos α=12 cell. The wider SAE's feature is a strictly better steering target across the magnitude range.

- The frontier is well-defined and steep: at coherence ≥ 2.5 you can get up to ~2.5% kw; demanding coherence ≥ 3 caps you at 1.3% kw. To go above ~3% kw you give up substantial coherence.

#### Where TXC would still help (indirect evidence)

`feat_7792` is the SAE's most-selective backtracking-precursor feature (Welch t-stat 10.8, mean(D₊)/mean(D) ≈ 5.3 across 1357 D₊ positions vs 97k D positions). It captures the signal in a single per-token feature; the per-prompt success rate is therefore an upper bound on what a per-token SAE can do here.

The TXC's hypothesised advantage is that its window-encoder represents a T-token context as one feature instead of T separate per-position features. Two pieces of indirect evidence support that hypothesis on this task:

- **`sum_top3` and `sum_top5` (multi-feature additive) don't help.** If the backtracking signal lived in a temporal pattern that a per-token SAE has to spread across multiple features, summing more features should improve performance. Instead, top-1 (80% success) > top-3 (40%) > top-5 (33%) at α=4. The signal IS concentrated in a single per-token feature, which is consistent with the SAE having found an unusually clean direction (at the cost of being just one direction) — but it also means a TXC could plausibly find *the same* feature with even higher selectivity by aggregating its T positions into one window-feature.

- **Multi-position steering changes the failure mode for the SAE but not for raw DoM.** Multipos α=12 has very different per-prompt distribution than every-position α=12 (bimodal vs uniform-collapsed) for the SAE feature, but barely changes anything for raw DoM (multipos α=16 ≈ 13.9%/coh=1.0, every-position α=12 ≈ 14.4%/coh=1.0 — same regime). This is consistent with the SAE feature being temporally localised; TXC's window encoder should naturally exploit that.

These are *suggestive*, not direct demonstrations. Direct demonstration requires training a TXC on Llama L10 — blocked tonight by the HF gate.

#### Important held-out-set caveat

The 30 held-out prompts are `PROMPTS[-N_HELDOUT:]` and the prompt set is generated category-by-category, so the held-out set is **30 prompts all from the "invariant" category** (parity arguments, chessboard tilings, monovariants in transformations). The 80% SAE success rate is therefore on this single category — it might not transfer to logic puzzles or geometry problems. Tomorrow's first follow-up should re-distribute the held-out set across all 10 categories (3 per category) and re-run the headline comparison.

Run `huggingface-cli login` once on h100_2 with a Meta-approved token. This unblocks every TXC / TSAE training experiment from the user's stretch list.

#### 3. Build Llama L10 activation cache at 3 hook points (~3-6h compute)

`build_llama_finetune_cache.py` is committed and tested. Three runs needed:

```bash
# All from /root/temp_xc-backtracking on h100_2 with TMPDIR + HF_HOME set:
.venv/bin/python -m experiments.phase7_unification.case_studies.backtracking.build_llama_finetune_cache \
    --hook resid --layer 10 --num-windows 100000 --batch-size 32
.venv/bin/python -m experiments.phase7_unification.case_studies.backtracking.build_llama_finetune_cache \
    --hook ln1   --layer 10 --num-windows 100000 --batch-size 32
.venv/bin/python -m experiments.phase7_unification.case_studies.backtracking.build_llama_finetune_cache \
    --hook attn  --layer 10 --num-windows 100000 --batch-size 32
```

100k windows × 128 ctx ≈ 13M tokens per cache, ~100 GB on disk per cache (so ~300 GB total — fits on `/workspace`).

#### 4. Train one TXC variant on Llama L10 (the one most likely to demonstrate temporal advantage)

Once at least the `attn` cache exists, train a TXC at `T=5, k_pos=100` on it. The Phase 7 trainer needs a parametrised path for Llama (`d_in=4096`); ~1h of code work to add a `--cache-dir` argument that bypasses the hardcoded Gemma cache and a `--d-in` override. Then ~2-3h training. Run the existing backtracking pipeline against the new ckpt.

#### 5. Transcoder variant: read ln1 → reconstruct attn_out

This is genuinely novel — most SAEs/TXCs reconstruct what they read. A transcoder predicts a *different* hook's output from the input hook. For interpretability of attention, this is the right shape. Two caches needed (ln1 + attn from step 3). The architecture is a TXC with `W_dec` shape mismatch; ~2h to write.
